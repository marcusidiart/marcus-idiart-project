Why I Love Golf

Golf found me early in life, and I haven't been able to shake it since. There's something uniquely humbling about a sport where every single swing is yours alone — no teammate to bail you out, no clock to run down. Just you, the club, and 400 yards of grass standing between you and the hole.

I play whenever I get the chance — weekend rounds with friends, twilight solo walks, the occasional scramble tournament. Beyond the competition, golf teaches me lessons I carry into everything else: stay composed under pressure, commit to the shot, and always be willing to reset after a bad hole.

Golf Fundamentals

Here's how I break down the core pillars of the game, in order of importance:

  1. The Mental Game
    • Stay in the present — one shot at a time
    • Visualize the shot before you swing
    • Accept bad holes and move on quickly
    • Manage expectations, especially on difficult courses
  2. The Grip and Setup
    • Neutral grip pressure — firm but not tight
    • Ball position changes with each club
    • Stance width should match shoulder width for irons
    • Consistent pre-shot routine builds muscle memory
  3. Ball Striking
    • Keep your head still through impact
    • Rotate hips before the arms come through
    • Strike down on the ball with irons (hit it then the turf)
    • Sweep the ball with driver — don't dig
  4. The Short Game (where scores are made)
    • Chipping: use the bounce of the wedge, not the leading edge
    • Pitching: accelerate through the ball, never decelerate
    • Bunkers: open the face, hit the sand two inches behind the ball
    • Putting: read the green from behind the hole, not behind the ball
  5. Course Management
    • Play to your strengths, not the scorecard yardage
    • Aim for the fat part of the green on tough pins
    • Know when to lay up instead of going for it
    • Keep a shot in play rather than gambling on hero shots

⛭ Driving

Prioritize fairways over distance. A straight 230-yard drive beats a 280-yard drive into the rough every time.

🎯 Putting

Speed control matters more than line. Most three-putts come from leaving the first putt 10 feet short or past the hole.

🎿 Practice

Spend 60% of range time on wedges and short irons. They're used the most and improve scores the fastest.
